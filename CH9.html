These two questions bring out the carelessness with which the topic of "damage to lunar photos" was treated. The basic mistake consists in the fact that the questions clearly speak of "lunar films and photos" and instead American Moon shows instead of films?... OF SCANS. A film or a photo is something physical and cannot be modified with impunity, while a scan is a set of intangible data and can be modified in a thousand different ways by software that is within everyone's reach. The author, in addition to confusing digital scans with celluloid films, has assumed that the scans that NASA has shared, in the Apollo Lunar Surface Journal >were unmodified originals without reading an important disclaimer that contains these sentences. Many of the scans of photos taken during the missions were done from the original film. These scans are being done by NASA Johnson, with some post-processing by Kipp Teague. The film is scanned at 4096 x 4096 pixels per image. (See a discussion from Arizona State University about the scanning process.) Kipp reduced each digital image to approximately 2350 x 2350 pixels (equivalent to 300 dpi) and did minor adjustments of levels to ensure that (1) brightly lit areas of lunar soil were neutral grey, (2) objects with known colors (such as the CDR stripes or the LCRU blankets) looked right, and (3) information in bright or dark areas was not lost. 29 - Since this is the result of the impact of cosmic rays on the film inside the magnetosphere, where the radiation is attenuated compared to open space, can you explain why in the lunar photos you don't notice any damage due to radiation? 30 - Given that the lunar surface is hit by an average of one to 4 particles per second per square centimeter, and that the cameras have been on the lunar surface for up to eight consecutive hours without any protection, can you explain why there are no signs of degradation on the film due to radiation?
Question 29 is totally meaningless and shows us a nuclear emulsion under a microscope obtained from a probe balloon, therefore well below the magnetosphere and inside the atmosphere. The real question then would be: why don't these cosmic rays that have passed the magnetosphere and penetrated the atmosphere destroy all the terrestrial films, given that the films on Earth are there for an indefinite time and not a few hours as on the Moon? The answer is this: cosmic rays meeting the atmosphere generate muons, which are extremely penetrating particles and which impress nuclear emulsions even at ground level, so much so that it was possible to use them to take an X-ray of the pyramid of Cheops. Why don't these particles that impress nuclear emulsions ruin films on Earth? Exactly for the same reason that lunar films have not been damaged by cosmic rays. These particles are too small and as I explained in this video to be able to generate a hole on the film of only a tenth of a millimeter, even if the rays were all helium nuclei, the most massive, and those that fall in one cm² all fell around the same point, it would take more than 1200 years, which is the time needed to fill a hole of one square decimillimeter with 152 billion helium atoms all concentrated and distributed over the surface of 0.1 mm², at the rate of 4 atoms per second. Nuclear emulsions, on the other hand, are composed with special reagents that ionize when cosmic rays pass through and from that ionized trace the presence of the ray is obtained under the microscope. In other words, those stripes are not cosmic rays, but are the ionized traces that they could have left in a material that was predisposed to interact with the cosmic ray. This muon detection system has even made it possible to x-ray the pyramid of Cheops, but it is evident that this interaction visible under the microscope in nuclear emulsions has nothing to do with photographic films that even on Earth are constantly crossed by muons. To further verify that the radiation during the journey to the Moon would not have irreparably damaged the films, here is this graph taken from the Soviet Zond 7 mission. As we can see from the graph, we have a consistent measurement of protons per square centimeter inside the spacecraft's cockpit, but the panchromatic film photo of the mission made to Earth well beyond the magnetosphere by Zond 7, a spacecraft that landed after its mission, shows no obvious radiation damage. (and if there were they were remediable and were corrected in the scan, just like for the photos of the Apollo missions). The questions then ask why the typical granulation from radiation pollution is absent on lunar films. We now know that greenish granulation is present in the dark areas, but only in the raw scans, particularly on the edges of the film, in the sky and in the shadows on the ground and of course it has been cleaned up in the scans edited by Tipp Teague. Lacking granulation in the bright areas, this cannot be confused with the pixelation of the scan, which is much smaller, at least in full resolution scans and that to be seen it is necessary to enlarge the image much more. Granulation is most likely the result of the bremsstrahlung effect, (and here we answer question 30), which is produced when a metal surface is hit by low-intensity corpuscular radiation such as that of the solar wind whose particles, when there are no solar flares, have a rather low energy, between 1 and 4 kev, corpuscular radiation that can be shielded with aluminum foil of a tenth of a millimeter. For this reason, on the Moon the camera chassis perfectly protected the films from the solar wind, but by braking the particles, they produced a weak X-ray radiation (bremsstrahlung effect) which, together with the cosmic radiation, less intense than the solar wind, but more penetrating and above all present throughout the mission, polluted the photos, in particular the dark areas of the film, such as the edges, the sky and the shadows on the ground. In conclusion: the damage to the lunar photos is certainly present and if American Moon has not shown it it is because it has consulted only admittedly modified scans, while on the raw scans present in the general NASA database there is degradation, even if today with image editing software, it is perfectly restorable as was done for the scans shown in the pages of the Apollo Lunar Surface Journal. The fact that NASA only shows restored scans and not the raw ones testifies to its good faith, as in the event of false landings, NASA would certainly have published the "irradiated" scans precisely to accredit a fake damage from space radiation, hiding if anything the real raw scans, i.e. the compromising and damage-free ones made by professional photographers on the set of the deception and this obviously to remove any suspicions as much as possible.
