10-Since this is the ascent engine of the LEM tested on earth, why is there no visible flame under it, when it starts again from the moon?

The tenth question again ignores a fundamental and general concept, namely that of never comparing phenomena that take place in the atmosphere with those that are produced in a vacuum of space, just as no sensible person would compare phenomena that are produced in the atmosphere with those that are produced in a liquid such as water. A gas released from its container expands, but while in the atmosphere it is held back by the presence of 3 27 billion billion molecules for every cm, on the Moon the gases are free to expand and the they do so at speeds unthinkable here on Earth. Here we see the Crew-3 spacecraft hit by the sun and we see pulses of gas coming out of its directional engines, also made visible, and expanding as if they were rays of light. This very rapid expansion, impossible in the atmosphere, produces a drastic drop in temperature, a drop that in gases is regulated by the law PV=nRT. This law is the one that makes the fridge, freezer and air conditioners work, where the "cold" is produced by the decompression of a gas, freon. This is compressed and liquefied, then it is introduced into an expansion chamber so that from liquid (where it occupies a small volume at high pressure) it passes to gaseous, (where it occupies a larger volume at lower pressure) and in doing so produces a net cooling, up to several degrees below zero. For this obvious reason, the flames coming from the engines, even those much hotter and more powerful than those of the LEM, inevitably disappear into the void, as we see from this image of a Starship engine in Earth orbit. Youtube The nozzle is incandescent so it is not turned off, in fact you can see the flames licking the edge of the nozzle, smoke comes out violently from the engine almost perpendicularly, but the plume of flames that the author of question N.10 would expect, is not there I imagine the usual conspiracy objections: but that rocket is not hypergolic, the example does not apply! Thus demonstrating all their ignorance of physical laws, as if the disappearance of the flames depended on the type of fuel and not on the temperature of the gases violently expanded in the vacuum. So let's put this gif where you can see the second stage of the Chinese rocket, the Long March 3B, which left to deposit a geostationary satellite. Here in the images you can see the activation of the second stage that works with hydrazine, so it is hypergolic, as can be seen from the initial sheet, and although the animation on the left considers it in thrust, the camera that frames the engines does not show any flame. Long_March_3B The violent lowering of the pressure in the vacuum with the consequent lowering of the temperature of the incandescent gases, makes these gases pass under the draper point which is the temperature below which the human eye does not perceive brightness, and which corresponds to about 525 degrees Celsius and this takes away from our sight the vision of the flames . Draper_point However, that the LEM engine was equipped with a flame can be seen in the so-called transients, which are very short moments that occur when the engine is turned on, where the flames are visible because combustion takes place initially and partially outside the nozzle, as we can see in this image of the restart of the three LEMs. Not only that, but in Apollo 17 you can see the flame inside the nozzle as soon as the LEM is in the optimal position.

11 - Since, as the debunkers themselves confirm, the astronauts are "literally sitting on the engine" inside the cabin, why do you not hear anything during the restart?

Once again we are faced with a question generated by profound ignorance both on how the various phases of the missions were carried out, and on the characteristics of the technical equipment that was used during the missions, from lift-offs to moon landings, passing through EVA (Extra Vehicular Activity). All departures from Earth as well as from the Moon took place for safety reasons with the pressurized suit and the use of the "bubble helmet" (polycarbonate helmet without helmet cover) and already the sealed helmet isolated the microphone from environmental noise. Here the three Apollo 11 astronauts who are preparing for launch and as we see have already worn the bubble helmet. But that's not all... with the helmet on and the suit pressurized, the VOX (Voice Over eXchange) system had to be implemented to communicate even between nearby astronauts as well as with Houston, with the same scheme with which communications were carried out during the EVAs. The system worked like this: the astronaut's microphone was activated only when the astronaut spoke and the same system deactivated it an instant after the last word, to avoid the continuous re-entry from the astronaut's microphone of the Houston voice that filtered through the speakers of the snoopy cap, worn under the helmet: For this reason it was impossible to hear external noises except when the astronauts were talking, but at that point the noise, certainly not deafening from the engine of the ascent module in the void, was very difficult to pick up under the voice of the astronaut with the microphone hermetically sealed in a helmet and 3 cm from the mouth. However, to confirm what is said, in one particular case the noises of the restart of the ascent module could be heard, albeit in the background, and this could have happened during the restart of Apollo 16. In fact, astronaut Duke had already had a problem with the VOX system from the first EVA that had not been enabled, so noises and all the echoes of Houston entered from his microphone during the EVA even when he was not speaking. We remember that the Vox system was used precisely to prevent every sentence pronounced by Houston from returning to Earth passing through the astronaut's microphone, in a continuous and annoying echo. Even when restarting from the Moon, Duke's VOX system was disabled, in fact by clicking on the link to the video below, you can hear for the first time the explosion of the charges that disconnected the base of the LEM from the ascent module, and further on you can hear a hiss that increases in frequency and which is the noise of the module's engine in progressive thrust. Youtube However, this anomaly of the VOX system perfectly explains the reason for the total silence of all the other restarts with the VOX system in operation. 12 - Since during the restart of Apollo 15 you can even hear the music coming from the recorder in the cabin, why is the engine not heard at all? 13 - The departure from the moon is perhaps the most delicate moment of the whole mission. Astronauts must maintain maximum concentration, and must be able to communicate with each other instantaneously, in case something goes wrong. So why put your safety at risk, playing music in the cabin at that very moment, thus risking losing concentration and not being able to communicate clearly with each other, in case of emergency?

The issue of the Apollo 15 restart music has been known for some time, and it was enough to read up: the music came from Worden's microphone which was in the command module and not on the LEM, and unlike his companions, not having a helmet he was able to bring a cassette player close to his microphone and play the music. We also remember that the command/service module orbited the Moon and did not need to keep the engines running, so there was a dead silence in the cabin, which is why no other noises can be heard. We also know that the music did not only reach Houston, where it was recorded, but also to the ears of his two companions departing from the Moon and this was probably not Worden's intention, at least if we stick to his statements. HERE (at 171:37:25) Let's read a few sentences of the whole issue and the controversy that followed. Worden in a 1999 statement said:- "I must confess that I played the song during the moon liftoff. I thought only Houston listened to it. But then I found out that someone had turned on the switch that transmitted my voice to the Lunar Module. So, Dave and Jim had to go through the pre-launch checklist with the song in their ears. Dave wasn't too happy about it, but I didn't know at the time that Houston had activated my radio circuit. I actually had this in mind during the flight, and I went ahead and played it during the ascent from the Moon. Jones: "I assume that you had a little tape recorder on board..." Scott: "Al did. He shouldn't have started it before a minute after takeoff, but that's how it went. From the long description in the Apollo Journal >we know that Worden brought to the control module a small personal cassette player with the music recorded, and we also know that the recorders by Edge by LEM e CM, (DSE-Data Storage Equipment) that here Let's see have only recording functions and not playback, and the constantly recorded audio was periodically downloaded from Houston, plus the recorders worked on continuous tape and not cassettes, so that little music could only come from the CM, where Worden was silent, he didn't have a helmet and he had equipped himself with a personal portable cassette recorder. The sentence: "The Falcon climbs off-camera, just as 'Air Force Song' can be heard from the onboard tape recorder", to which the author appeals, says only that the music did not come from Houston, but from a recorder brought on board, but certainly not from the DSE, which as we have said, had no playback functions.
